Six months ago I started writing three pages of longhand every morning. No structure, no topic, no audience. Just whatever comes out.

I stole the idea from Julia Cameron's *The Artist's Way*, but I've adapted it for my brain — which is a developer brain that wants to optimize everything, including the practice of not optimizing.

## The rules

1. Write first thing in the morning, before checking your phone
2. Write three pages (or about 15 minutes if typing)
3. Don't edit, don't re-read, don't judge
4. There is no wrong way to do this

```
fix stuff
update
wip
final version
final version 2
ok actually final
```

Most mornings, the first page is garbage. Complaints about sleep, random anxieties, grocery lists. But somewhere on page two, something interesting usually shows up. A connection I hadn't made. A solution to a problem I'd been stuck on. A project idea that actually excites me.

## Why it works for developers

Programming is structured thinking. Morning pages are the opposite — unstructured, messy, nonlinear. That contrast matters.

When I code all day, my brain gets stuck in logic mode. Everything becomes an if/else. Morning pages break that pattern. They let the associative, creative part of my brain run for a few minutes before the analytical part takes over.

Some of my best architectural decisions have come from morning pages. Not because I was *trying* to solve a technical problem, but because I gave my brain space to wander near the problem without pressure.

## What I've noticed

After six months:

- **I make decisions faster.** Writing forces you to articulate what you're actually thinking, which reveals what you actually believe.
- **I worry less.** Getting anxious thoughts on paper makes them feel manageable instead of overwhelming.
- **I have more ideas.** Not all good, but the volume increased dramatically. Ideas seem to breed when you give them a place to live.
- **I write better code comments and docs.** Daily writing practice makes all writing easier.

## The resistance

Every productivity-minded developer I've told about this has the same reaction: "I don't have time." I get it. But here's the math — 15 minutes is 1% of your waking hours. And the clarity you get back makes the other 99% more effective.

The real resistance isn't time. It's discomfort. Sitting with your own thoughts, unfiltered, is genuinely uncomfortable. Your brain will try everything to avoid it — suddenly you need to check Slack, or you remember an urgent email, or you decide this is a good time to reorganize your desk.

Push through. The discomfort is the point.

## How to start

Tomorrow morning, before you open your laptop, open a notebook or a blank text file. Set a timer for 15 minutes. Write whatever comes to mind. Don't stop, don't edit, don't think about it too hard.

Do it for a week. If it doesn't help, stop. But I think you'll be surprised.
